A story about what we lost

The Closing Window

5.6 billion people now use social media.
We have almost no ability to study it.

Scroll to begin

For nearly two decades, social media platforms were among the most studied phenomena in modern science. Open APIs, research partnerships, and free tools gave thousands of academics, journalists, and civil society groups an unprecedented window into how information spreads, how opinions form, and how billions of people interact online.

Then, in the span of just 18 months, that window slammed shut.

Chapter 1

The Golden Age

2006 – 2020

When Twitter launched in 2006, it did something unusual: it made its data available. The platform's API let anyone — developers, researchers, journalists — access the public stream of tweets flowing across the network. Facebook's Graph API soon followed, giving researchers access to public pages, groups, and social connections.

What emerged was an extraordinary ecosystem for studying human behavior at scale.

0 academic studies used Twitter data between 2006 and 2024, spanning 16 disciplines and generating over 610,000 citations.

Platforms didn't just tolerate research — they actively supported it. They built partnerships, funded studies, and created dedicated tools for understanding their own impact on the world.

What We Learned

This access didn't just produce papers — it produced understanding. Landmark studies that shaped public policy, congressional testimony, and global awareness of social media's impact were only possible because researchers could actually see what was happening on these platforms.

2018

False news travels 6x faster

An MIT study of 126,000 Twitter cascades found that falsehoods were 70% more likely to be retweeted and reached the first 1,500 people six times faster than true stories.

Vosoughi, Roy & Aral — Science
2019

People over 65 shared 7x more fake news

By linking survey data to Facebook sharing behavior, researchers discovered that users over 65 shared seven times more misinformation than younger users during the 2016 election.

Guess, Nagler & Tucker — Science Advances
2023

The 2020 Election studies

A landmark collaboration between Meta and 17 external researchers produced four major papers in Science and Nature studying Facebook and Instagram's effects on elections.

Stroud, Tucker et al. — Science & Nature
2021

Instagram's effect on teen mental health

Whistleblower Frances Haugen revealed Facebook's internal research showing 13.5% of UK teen girls reported suicidal thoughts becoming more frequent after starting Instagram.

Facebook internal research, leaked documents

These weren't abstract academic exercises. They informed congressional hearings, shaped public understanding of misinformation, and drove global conversations about social media's role in elections, mental health, and democracy.

And then it all started to disappear.

Chapter 2

The Doors Close

2018 – 2024

The first blow came in 2018. After the Cambridge Analytica scandal revealed that a researcher had exploited Facebook's open API to harvest data on 87 million users, the company locked down its platform. It was an understandable response to a real abuse — but the restrictions went far beyond closing the loophole. Facebook effectively shut the door on most independent research.

Other platforms watched. And one by one, they followed.

A Timeline of Closing Doors

March 2018

Cambridge Analytica Scandal

Facebook massively restricts its Graph API after it's revealed that researcher Aleksandr Kogan's app harvested data on 87 million users. Academic researcher Axel Bruns calls it the "APIcalypse."

August 2021

Facebook Shuts Down NYU Researchers

Facebook disables the accounts of NYU researchers studying political ad targeting — hours after they informed Facebook they were investigating the spread of January 6th disinformation.

February 2023

Twitter Kills Free API Access

Under Elon Musk's ownership, Twitter ends free API access. The new Enterprise tier costs $42,000 per month — for less data than researchers previously received for free. Over 100 research projects are immediately canceled or halted.

$0 → $42,000/mo
April 2023

Reddit Announces API Pricing

After 15 years of free API access, Reddit announces it will charge $12,000 per 50 million API calls. Popular third-party apps like Apollo (cost: ~$20M/year) are forced to shut down. Over 8,500 subreddits go dark in protest.

Free for 15 years → $20M/yr
August 2024

Meta Shuts Down CrowdTangle

Meta permanently shuts down CrowdTangle — the primary tool used by researchers, journalists, and election monitors to understand how content spreads on Facebook and Instagram. Its replacement, the Meta Content Library, is described by critics as having "1% of the features."

~100,000 users lost access
0 of surveyed researchers said CrowdTangle's shutdown would hinder their work
0 research projects canceled or halted after Twitter's API paywall
0 decline in published Twitter research in just the first year

In 18 months — from February 2023 to August 2024 — the three platforms that had been the backbone of social media research all cut off access. Twitter priced out academics. Reddit ended 15 years of free access. Meta killed the most widely-used transparency tool in the industry.

The tools researchers had relied on didn't just become more expensive. They ceased to exist.

Chapter 3

The Paradox

More users than ever, less understanding than ever

Here is the uncomfortable truth at the center of this story: social media has never been larger, never been more influential, and never been harder to study.

Global Social Media Users vs. Research Access

As platforms grew to reach billions, they systematically cut off the ability to study them.

In 2010, there were 970 million social media users worldwide and researchers had essentially free, open access to the major platforms. By 2025, there are 5.66 billion users — nearly six times more — and researchers have effectively lost access to all of them.

Chapter 4

What's at Stake

The consequences of flying blind

Social media platforms are now the primary information environment for most of the world's population. They shape elections, influence public health decisions, amplify or suppress speech, and affect the mental health of billions — including children.

And we are studying them less than at any point in the last 15 years.

We can't track how misinformation spreads during elections. We can't measure the impact of algorithmic changes on public discourse. We can't study how young people are affected by the content they see. We can't hold platforms accountable for the promises they make because we can't independently verify anything.

The platforms that most shape public life have become the least studied institutions in modern society.

Elections

We can no longer independently monitor how misinformation spreads during the most consequential democratic moments. Researchers who tracked election integrity in 2020 cannot replicate that work today.

Public Health

During COVID, researchers could track health misinformation in real time using platform APIs. The next public health crisis will unfold with far less visibility into how false claims spread.

Youth & Mental Health

Teen depression rates have doubled since 2011. But the internal research that revealed Instagram's impact on teen mental health only came to light through a whistleblower — not through independent study.

Accountability

Platforms routinely make claims about their safety efforts, content moderation, and algorithmic fairness. Without independent access, there is no way to verify any of it.

The story of social media research is a story about something rare being built — and then dismantled. An entire ecosystem of tools, partnerships, and open data that took over a decade to create was undone in under two years.

But it doesn't have to end here.

Legislation like the Platform Accountability and Transparency Act and the EU's Digital Services Act are beginning to mandate researcher access. In December 2025, the European Commission fined X 120 million euros — in part for failing to provide researchers with data access.

The question isn't whether we need to study social media. The question is whether we'll build the infrastructure to do it — independent of any single platform's willingness to cooperate.

The window is closing. But it doesn't have to stay shut.

New tools, new approaches, and new coalitions are emerging to rebuild our ability to study the platforms that shape public life. The next chapter of this story is being written now.
